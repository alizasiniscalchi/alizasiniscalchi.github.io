Week 2: Shaper.

For this weeks project, we created a desk organizer using a CNC Router, the Shaper.

To start off my porject, I first had to sign up to be traianed on the Shaper, as I have never used it before. Upon arriving for my appointment, Madison, my Professor and Trainer, walked me through the steps of creating a design in Adobe to be cut on the Shaper. We then went over the, extremely important, machine safety steps.

Before you can start working on the machine, you have to save your Adobe Illustrator file to the shaper thumb drive, which can be plugged directly into the shaper machine. In order to safely cut on your wood stock, you must secure it to the board by applying a strong double sided tape, and screwing the wood into the workspace, so that the top of your wood is flat (no screw heads sticking out).

Prior to cutting your design, you have to map your workspace. To do this, you move the shaper around your workspace so that you have scanned your whole stock, while also always having the special mapping tape in view, so that each square of the mapping tape turns blue on the shaper screen, meaning it is fully mapped.

We learned that it is always important to plug the vacuum into the machine, so that it collects the wood being cut from your board, as to avoid it flying off your project, or built up debris getting in the way of your work. It is also important to stop periodically in between the different cuts in your project to vacuum the board to ensure a clean and smooth surface.

As previously mentioned, machine safety is very important. In the above picture, I would like to highlight the use of earplugs and safety goggles when the spindle is set to on and you are actively cutting.

To cap off my training, I reviewed the posted, Shaper Review video, before going into the lab to work on my final project, to refresh my memory on how the machine works.

There are a few really cool features, which are big parts of how the Shaper works, that I would like to highlight.

The first cool feature is mapping your workspace, which I have explained the process of above. This is very useful because it stores your workspace on the Shaper, meaning you could alwasy duplicate your project with ease, or, if necessary, finish your project another day.

The screen on the Shaper is vital to properly using the machine. The screen alows you to see and, map your workspace, locate the various places to cut on your workspace, calibrate the end mill using "z-touch," change the depth at which the end mill cuts, change the type of cut, and many other things we didn't even begin to touch upon.

The two bits we used for our project were, the V-Bit and the 1/4" downcut flat end mill. The V-Bit was used for cutting out our text and the flat end mill was used for our pocket cutting and cutting out our project. There is also a long tool change accessory, which is used to loosen the spindle, so you can remove it and change the end mill. After removing the spindle, you have to use the wrench to loosen and remove the end mill, and then to tighten the new end mill.

When cutting on the Shaper machine, remember to always have both hands on the handles and not to make any sudden movements that may throw off the cutting process. The green button on the right handle is used for cutting and the yellow/orange button on the left handle is for retracting the spindle.

When cutting out the final perimeter, it is important to only cut between 0.25 and 0.4 inches at a time, it may take a few laps around the perimeter. It is also very important to remember to leave two tabs on the last go around. If you dont leave tabs, your final product is subject to come flying off the workspace. After the project is complete, and the Shaper has been turned off and moved form the workspcae, you use a hammer and chisel to break the tabs, which you can then sand off.

To sketch my design, I utilized the rectangle and circle shape tools. I also used the filet tool on every rectangle corner, to round the edge, which was a stylistic choice. After talking to another student in the class, I learned that I had to return to sketch mode, and right click the sketch in the left browse bar and select "Save As DXF".

Week 3: FDM Printing.

For this weeks project, we created a cup, to fit into our desk organizer from last weeks project, using the FDM Printer, the Prusa MK3.

To begin my project, I first had to be trained on the Prusa MK3 FDM Printer. During my trianing, I was walked through the steps of designing and printing a small keychain. The first step was to design the keychain in Fusion360, as we learned how to use for our last project. Then we had to upload the design to Prusa Slicer, which I will explain later, and export that code to the SD card used for the printer.

To begin printing, make sure that the magnetic tray is in place, and add a layer of glue stick to the board if you are using a bigger project that may need help sticking. Before starting the project, the screen will display the temperature, which has to get to a certain point before begining. Using the dial, select unload filament to begin switching filament colors, and follow the on screen instructions. Similarly, twist the dial to load filament and follow the instructions until the nozzle dispenses your color. Then I was able to select my file and start printing my demo!

The next step was to start desigining my cup in Fusion360. The techniques are very similar to the techniques we learned and used while making the desk organizer, so I won't go into too much detail, but I will explain my process. I began by creating the sketch with the base of my cup, which matched the diameter of the circle in my desk organizer plus 0.25" to account for the quarter inch end mill used on the Shaper. Then I extruded the base to the desired height.

To create a more bulbus shape, rather than a straight cylinder, I had to create 2 new planes. On those planes I added circles of my desired diameter, to shape the cup.

The next step is to loft your project, which follows the same concept as extruding it. But, lofting brings the shapes up to each plane, to create the bulbus cup shape. After lofting the cup, I realized that I was not happy with the size of the circle on the second plane, so I went in and edited that sketch. I also measured the pens I planed on putting in the cup, and I realized I wanted the cup to be taller, so I raised the height of the top plane.

I then had to decide how I wanted to design the exterior of my cup. I started scrolling through different shapes and sketches in Fusion360, until I stumbled upon the Torus tool. I was drawn to this design because it lays horizontally on the cup, rather than vertically, which is the more common design I have seen. Using the torus tool, I added a ring to my cup and adjusted the diameter and thicknes to fit along my cup. I repeated this process all the way up the cup.

To finish my cup design, I added a shell, which basically hollows out my shape to make it a useable cup. Then I filetd the brim of the cup to give it a softer appearance that matched the rings.

After exporting my Fusion360 design as an STL file, in my finder screen, I right clicked on the file and pressed open with PrusaSlicer. My computer then opened PrusaSlicer, with my project on a mock up of the magnetic printer map. In the right side bar, I changed the print settings to 0.20mm SPEED, which is an appropriate setting to achieve quality and some level of time efficiency. I also confirmed that the filament type selected was Prusament PLA, which matched the filament I was using, and the machine it was set to was the MK3. I set infill to 10%, which determines how hollow the project will print, mine didn't have to be very dense because it is just a pen holder.

Once all of my settings were to my liking, I exported the gcode and saved it to the SD card used for the FDM printers. Using the same steps to print as I did for the demo keychain I began printing my project.

Week 4: SLA Printing.

For this weeks project I created a ring using the SLA Printer. Unfortunately, we were not permitted to use the SLA Printers, due to the fact that we would have such little training time and the resin used in the printer is dangerous upon contact. It was thought that we would need more time than possible to be properly trained. So, I was able to do my own research into how the printer works, and I designed a braided ring, by following a provided video, and sent my design to my TA to have it printed.

SLA (Stereolithography) Printing is a form of 3D Printing that uses resin to create the 3D object. The printer creates the 3D object layer by layer, by using a laser to harden the liquid resin.

Through researching the SLA printers, I learned the basic procedure for using and handling the printer. It is very important to wear gloves throughout the entire process, because if resin comes in contact with the skin it can cause a serious rash. If you have to change the resin cartridge, make sure you put in a clean resin basin that is programmed to the color of the resin cartridge. Clean the design plate with isopropyl to assure it is completely clean from old resin. After reassembling the printer and after your design is prepared with supports, you can then send it to the printer to begin printing. After the printer is complete, you can remove the 3D object using a spatula and add it to the cleaning machine and set it to the specific settings of your resin. This step will remove any excess resin from your project and make it safe to touch. After it has finished cleaning, add the object to the curing machine and set it to the settings for your resin. This final step is what allows the resin to cure, or fully harden. Once it is done, you can take it out and cut off the supports, which is the step in which I was able to takeover at.

To design my actual ring, I followed the Bradided Ring tutorial, which was posted on our classes website, and is also linked below. A pro to this video would be that the actual methods used were not very difficult. A major con to this video is that the creator worked so quickly and his measurements seemed so random that it was hard to replicate. I will talk about the problem this caused me a little further down.

The first new tool I learned while creating my braided ring was the Pipe tool. To use the pipe tool, you first create a linear design and then select the design and select pipe, which creates a cylindrical shape around whatever linear design was selected.

The flange tool was the next new tool I learned. This tool was under the sheet metal tab. After researching the tool it seems to be used to create a face and bend attached to an existing face. However, when we used it, it almost seemed similar to using the extruding tool.

The next new tool was so simple, yet a tool I never would have expected to find. After using the flange tool on the arc, I was able to use the un-fold tool to completely starighten the arc.

To change the size of the pipe curvature so that 4 copies would fit on the unfolded arc, half of the circle(ring), I learned how to use the scale tool. I put the measurement of the unfolded arc, the number 4 which represents how many of the pipe I wanted to fit, and the measurement of the pipe. After pressing okay, my pipe design was successfully resized.

The next tool I learned was more of a technique and a repeat of one of the other new tools. For this part of the design, I created a square on the face of the arc and extruded it to create a box. Then I extruded it downwards to join it to the pipe curvature. After the two were connected, I was able to refold the arc, and with it the pipe curvature. I was able to refold the arc with the pipe by creating a bridge between the two of them using the block.

The one part of the design process that I really struggled with was when, in the video, he added a triangle and extruded it down to cut the pipe. As I mentioned earlier, I tried my best to follwo his measurements, but he speed through it so quickly, I thought I had done the triangle right, however, my triangle was smaller than his. So, when I attempted to create a circular pattern and copy thr pipe, the pipes didnt all meet, creating a solid ring, becuase each segment was too short.

After going over my design with my TA and putting the YouTube video in 0.5x speed, we were able to recreate the exact measurements of the triangle and succesfully create the circular pattern, whcih we repeated twice to create the final braided ring design.

After our TA printed our designs, all that was left for me to do was pick it up from the lab and use clippers to snap off the supports. Then I had my finished braided ring!

Week 5: Vinyl Cutting.

For this weeks project I created a t-shirt design using Adobe Illustrator and Sure Cuts A Lot and used the vinyl cutter and heat press to create my final t-shirt!

In my opinion, vinyl cutting is a form of digital fabrication. To summarize the definition of digital fabrication, it refers to using manufacturing equipmet by way of digital data. So, for vinyl cutting, we created our design using Adobe Illustrator and Sure Cuts A Lot on our computers. By using these software platforms, we created digital data of our design, that we then sent to the vinyl cutting machine to tell it how to opperate. The vinyl cutter was able to cutt our design because of our digital file, which makes it digital fabrication.

A raster image is a image or photograph that contains many tiny pixels which come together to form the whole image. A vector image, on the other hand, uses a mathematical formula to form lines inbetween points to digitally render an image. While working in Adobe Illustrator, I selected an image and then selected "Image Trace" to convert the image from a rastor image to a vector image. This gave my image perceftly smooth, non-pixalated edges, which will be a easier and better looking when sent to the vinyl cutter.

I chose the 4 dog silhouettes, to represent my dogs. I had a chocolate lab, who recently passed away, represented by the first silhouette of the dog laying down, I have a yellow lab, represented by the second silhouette of the dog sitting, I have a resuce hound/lab/mut mix, represented by the silhouette of the third dog sitting, and I have a resuce pitbull/boxer mix represented by the fourth dog sitting. After scrolling through many dog silhouettes, I somehow found 4 that truly resembled my dogs! I knew that I wanted to do my puppies, because I jump at the chance to do anything related to dogs, so the most difficult part was coming up with a quote. I stumbled upon the quote, "Dogs are like chips... you can't have just one" and thought it was so cute and fititing since I have many pups. The quote and graphic fell into place quite easily since I knew my inspiration, so next came arranging it all on the shirt.

To begin my design, I started a new project in Illustrator and made the dimensions of my workspace to be an estimate of the dimensions of the t-shirt I plan to create. This way I can have a guide for sizing as I create my design.

I first added my quote, by simply using the text tool. I then played around with some different fonts and sizing until I was happy with the text format.

Eventhough I already had my graphics picked out, I decided I wanted to put those on the back with the text on the front. However, I felt like the front needed something else, so I added a graphic of four foot prints to represent the four dogs. After inserting the image, I selected "Image Trace" to turn it into a vector image. Then I selected "Expand" to gain more options, where I then selected "Isolate Image," which allowed me to delete the parts of the image I did not want. To finalize the front design, I just moved the paw prints into place.

I then saved my front design as a .png so I could open it on Sure Cuts A Lot in the lab. I also took a screenshot to get an idea of the dimensions of my front graphic.

To create the back design, I cleared my workspace, becuase I already had a copy of the front design saved, and it was easy to re-use the measurements. Then I repeated the steps I used to insert the paw print graphic on the front, for all four of my dog graphics. Again, those steps are, paste image onto workspace, select "Image Trace," select "Expand," select "Isolate Image," remove any unwanted parts of the graphic, and then resize the graphic.

I then saved my back design as a .png so I could open it on Sure Cuts A Lot in the lab. I also took a screenshot to get an idea of the dimensions of my back graphic.

Once I got into the lab, I turned the vinyl cutter on and to the appropriate setting for cutting apparel vinyl. Then I had to add my front graphic in to the software, Sure Cuts A Lot, on the designated computer. I adjusted the sizing to make sure it fit the t-shirt I had taken from the cabinet. The most important step is to remember to flip the graphic horizontally since you flip the vinyl over to transfer it. I then pressed "Cut with Cutter" and sent my design to the vinyl cutter. I repeated these steps with the back design.

After all of my design had been cut onto the vinyl, I had to weed out my design. The weeding process refers to removing all of the excess/background vinyl, that is not part of my design. This includes the spaces in between letters and anything else you wouldn't want on your final shirt.

Now that my design was all ready to be transferred to my shirt, I had to turn the heating press on and wait until the temperature reached high 300s, about 370 degrees farenheit. I pre-pressed my shirt to remove the wrinkles, and then began placing my front design. After pressing my front design for 10 seconds, I flipped the shirt over and placed and pressed my back design for 10 seconds.

After both sides were fully pressed I was able to peel of the plastic and reveal my final t-shirt!

Week 6: Laser Cutting.

For this weeks project, I created a heart shaped box, with parametric kerfing, on the laser cutter.

The design process for this week was actually quite simple. We used a website, provided by our teacher, to generate the design for our box. Upon opening the website, I selected the type of box I wanted to create, the shape, and then it opened a form where I could change the dimensions and such.

To gain a better idea of the size I was working with, I converted 19in. (the width of our wood) into mm. I then typed this value, approximately 482mm, into the reference value for the box design to see if the layout would fit. After generating the box, I saw that the design was over the 482mm reference, so I proportionally decreased the width and height of the box design until it fit within the reference width.

After achieving the proper measurements, I saved my design as a .svg file.

The last step in my design process was to import my box design into Adobe Illustrator and add text. Upon adding the file to Illustrator, I deleted the reference box from the design, added text, and added a dragonfly for an additional design. Before exporting my file, I had to select the text and press "Create Outlines" which turns the text into a shape which can be cut on the laser cutter. I then saved my finished design file as a .svg.

When I arrived at the lab to begin cutting my project, I opened the website, app.glowforge.com, and uploaded my .svg file. This program connects right to the printer, which I then turned on.

I picked out a piece of birch plywood from the "Free to Use" drawer and placed it into the laser cutter. This drawer contains pre-approved material, since the laser cutter is a CO2 cutter and can only safely cut organic material. I then turned on the air filter, which reduces smoke, smell, and risk of fire.

On the glowforge site, I then set my focus around the engraving. I then pressed print on the glowforge site, which then displayed my time estimate, and I pressed the blue blinking start button on the laser cutter.

The laser cutter then began engraving and cutting. The whole process took a little under 20 minutes, in which I stood in the designated area and watched. The machine began by engraving the intitials and dragonfly, and then moved on to the cutting process.

After the laser has concluded cutting, the glowforge site states that the machine is cooling down and will then tell you when it is ready. After it finished cooling, I had my completed laser cut design. The last step was to construct the box by using the live hinges to bend around the arcs of the heart and piece the gridlocks together.

Once the pieces were all put together, I had my finished heart box!

I had also envisioned the idea for the company being like a high end bakery, but for dog food and treats. With this in mind, I selected "Daily Challenge" to write the name "Della's" in because I thought this font was cute and playful like Adella. I selected the font "Shorelines" for the word "delicacies" becuase it was sweet but in a more formal cursive, which reminded me of the font a high end bakery would use. Adella's coloring is very complex and has a mixture of dark brown shades, with some light brown/tan areas on her face. To resemble this in a simple icon, I used the darker brown on her ears and the lighter brown for the head shape. I have also proclaimed that her favorite color is pink and she has a pink collar, which is where the inspiration for light pink text comes in.

Once I arrived at the lab, I saved my file on an in lab computer as a .png. I opened the software Premier+ and started a new project.

On the blank project screen, I then chose the selections to Express Design in Hoop and continued with an Express Embroidery.

I then opened my specific file, removed the background, and was able to see a preview of my embroidered design. I then sent my design to mySewnet, which is a cloud based system that shares it directly with the embroidery machine.

To begin embroidering, I attached my outside square of material to a square of adhesive stabilizer and secured the two layers into the embroidery hoop that was compatible with the machine.

On the screen connected to the machine, I opened a new embroidery project, selected my file, and positioned my design on in the middle of the workspace.

After setting up the machine, by threading the upper thread and adding the bobbin, I pressed start and let the machine embroider. When prompted by the machine, I switched the thread and bobbin colors.

After some difficulty with the pink thread getting caught in the fabric, I had a finished embroidery design!

To begin sewing my pieces of fabric into a drawstring bag, I first started by sewing seams along the sides of the trim/drawstring channels. Then I sandwiched that in between an outside and an inside piece of fabric, clean sides towards each other, and sewed a seam at the top. I repeated this for the other set of fabric, that would make up the back.

Then, I sewed the inside piece to the inside piece with clean sides together, and the outside to the outside with clean sides together, making sure not to sew over the draqwstring channels, and leaving about a 3inch gap to flip the porject right side out. After attaching the two sides of the bag together and turning it right side out, I sewed the 3inch gap on the inside of the bag.

After braiding some yarn together to make a thicker drawstring and threading that through, I had a finished embroidered, drawstring bag!

Week 8: Integrate.

For this weeks project, I created an acrylic sign with working LED lights and a 3D printed base.

To begin, I had to come up with what design I wanted on my acrylic sign. As exemplified in past projects, I am practically obsessed with my dogs, so I decided I wanted to create another dog focused project. I came up with the idea of creating a spotify "curretly playing" image to engrave on the acrylic sign, but I had to figure out how to combine my two ideas. To combine both of my ideas, I created a Spotify playlist that contained songs about dogs, and designed a currently playing design that included the Spotify code that linked to my playlist.

To design my acrylic sign, I uploaded the provided acrylic file template to Adobe Illustrator. Ontop of the premeasured base, I added a rectangle with rounded edges to create the basic cutout to my design.

Using the rectangle, oval(circle), and polygon(triangle) tools, I created the basic design of the Spotify "currently playing" screen. I used the design of my 4 dogs from my "Week 5: Vinyl Cutting" project as the main "album cover." As the title and "artist's names" I used the name of my playlist "for my dogs" and the name of my 4 dogs, respectively. To prepare the text to be engraved on the laser cutter, I had to select all of the text and press "create outlines."

I had trouble getting a clear design for the spotify code by just simply adding the image to Illustrator. So, instead, after uploading the image, I used the paintbrush tool to trace the code, which created a higher quality design.

To finalize my acrylic sign design, I had to horizontally flip the whole design in preparation for engraving on the laser cutter.

Due to the fact that I was not able to get into the lab earlier to get an idea of how to design the 3D printed case, my first design was created on a whim. My main idea was to create a box which fit snuggly around the wooden pieces. Beyond that, I had no guidance. So, I basically printed my box just like that. Below is how that worked out for me.

The next major step was to design the case for my sign which would be fabricated on the 3D printer. To start my case design in Fusion 360, I collected the measurements from the provided wood cutout file, and rounded up to allow a very small amount of wiggle room when inserting the wooden pieces.

Similarly, the wooden pieces were designed to have rounded edges. So, to beable to fit the wooden pieces into the case, I obtained the radius of the edges on the Illustrator file, rounded it up, and fileted the edges on Fusion 360.

I then extruded the base up 2in. to ensure enough room for the three wooden pieces, LED lights, and circut board wires to fit.

Using the meausrement obtained from the Illustrator file, I added the rectangle used for the slot at the top of the cover. I then extruded the slot down to cut into the existing body.

I then realized that I created all of these measurements to the exact size of the wooden pieces, so if I were to shell the design, it would be too small. When playing with the shell settings, I realized that there was a way to shell to the outside, leaving my precise measurements intact. After, shelling the case, the extrusion of the slot was interrupted, so I had to re-extrude/re-cut the slot.

Using the obtained measurements which represent the whole that the plug will come through, I sketched a rectangle onto the case body. Using the extrusion/cut tool, I cut this hole into the body of the cover.

At this point, I had finished the design for my case, opened the STL file in PrusaSlicer, and exported my g-code after ensuring all of my settings were in place.

After exporting my g-code, I saved it onto an SD card to be pluged into one of the Prusa MK3 3D printers and began printing my base.

The next component of this project was creating the circut board to be used with the LED lights. This part of the project went over a little differently than normal. For the electronics training, instead of creating a small training project, we created our actual circut board durign our training.

Using the circut board design provided, we began the process of cutting and carving the pathways of the circut board onto a thin piece of plastic with a sheet of copper over it, using an SRM machine. We first set up the home setting on the associated software. We imported one of our design files, ran the machine, and repeated for our other 2 design files.

The soldering station that was setup by my trainor included a clamp, a heat gun, soldering paste, soldering metal, and a soldering iron.

Using the soldering paste, we placed the plug onto the circut board, and used the heat gun to melt the soldering paste in order to set the solder. This method of soldering was used to connect parts of the circut board that didn't need an extra level of strength. If extra strength was required, we used a different method which involved soldering metal and a soldering iron. To solder pieces such as the connectors and the wires, we placed the soldering metal near the are and heated it with the soldering iron until the metal had melted onto the area.

After testing all of the wires to ensure our circut board was soldered correctly, we had our completed circut board. To prepare the board to go in the base, I used scrap pieces of wood and a hot glue gun to stack the 2 wooden boards, so they didn't move around.

Before we could cut our design into our acrylic, we had to run a test print on scrap plywood. To run the laser cut, I used all of the steps outlined in "Week 6: Laser Cutting." After running a few tests, I still did not like the way the play button and spotify logo were printing. So, I received help form my teacher to correct the problem prior to printing on acrylic.

When I was happy with my design, I inserted my piece of acrylic and lined my design up. Becuase we were cutting on acrylic, we had to input custum cut and engrave settings in order for the cut to run smoothely. After the cut was complete, I had another step to the project complete! The one problem with this part of the project, was that the glowforge app used to print to the laser cutter, resized our Illustrator file, and we had not realized it until after the fact. To solve this problem, my teacher printed me a wooden piece with a smaller slot.

I assembeled my project by glueing the wooden slot to the top of the 3D printed cover and pusing the other two boards into the bottom of the case. To top it off, I inserted the acrylic sign. After observing my porject, I decided I needed something at the base of the cover to encase the wooden pieces.

To create the base for my 3D printed case, I opened my Fusion 360 file back up so I could work directly on the same workspace. To begin the design, I created a rectangle which extended 0.25 in. on all 4 sides of the original case. I added a filet to all 4 corners, with a radius that was proportionally bigger than the original corners.

I then performed 2 seperate extrusions to create the the base. I first extruded the base down to create a part of the base below the plane of the case. I then performed an upwards extrusion with a thickness of 0.23 in. in order to create a border around the base, to hold it in place.

To allow space for the cord to exit, I created a rectangle on the base wall and used the extrude tool to cut the shape out.

Once I had my final base design, I opened the STL file in PrusaSlicer, ensured all the correct settings were in, and exported my g-code to an SD card.

I was then able to print my base on the 3D printer. When the printer was done, I had the final component to my project!

Once I placed the 3D printed case into the base, with all of the wooden pieces, and the acrylic sign, I had my finished project. When I plugged it in, the LED light lit up green, which I thought was very fitting for my Spotify themed project with a green cover and base.
